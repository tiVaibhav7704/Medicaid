Tetanus

Tetanus is different from other vaccine-preventable diseases because it does not spread from person to person. The bacteria are usually found in soil, dust, and manure and enter the body through breaks in the skin — usually cuts or puncture wounds caused by contaminated objects.Today, tetanus is uncommon in the United States, with an average of about 30 reported cases each year. Nearly all cases of tetanus are among people who did not get all the recommended tetanus vaccinations. This includes people who have never received a tetanus vaccine and adults who don’t stay up to date on their 10-year booster shots.

Symptoms

The symptoms associated with tetanus include: 

                1.Jaw cramping
                2.Sudden, involuntary muscle tightening (muscle spasms) – often in the stomach
                3.Painful muscle stiffness all over the body
                4.Trouble swallowing
                5.Jerking or staring (seizures)
                6.Headache
                7.Fever and sweating
                8.Changes in blood pressure and fast heart rate

Cause

A bout of tetanus can be caused by a wide range of disorders, infections and events including: 

                1.Wounds contaminated with dirt, poop (feces), or spit (saliva)
                2.Wounds caused by an object puncturing the skin (puncture wounds), like a nail or needle
                3.Burns
                4.Crush injuries
                5.Injuries with dead tissue

Diagnosis of tetanus

Successful treatment depends on diagnosing the cause. Investigations may include: 

                1.Care in the hospital
                2.Immediate treatment with medicine called human tetanus immune globulin (TIG)
                3.Aggressive wound care
                4.Drugs to control muscle spasms
                5.Antibiotics
                6.Tetanus vaccination

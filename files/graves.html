Graves' disease is an autoimmune disorder that causes hyperthyroidism, or overactive thyroid.

With this disease, your immune system attacks the thyroid and causes it to make more thyroid hormone than your body needs.

The thyroid is a small, butterfly-shaped gland in the front of your neck.

Thyroid hormones control how your body uses energy, so they affect nearly every organ in your body—even the way your heart beats.

Symptoms

You may have common symptoms of grave's disease are such as

1.fast and irregular heartbeat.

2.frequent bowel movements or diarrhea.

3.heat intolerance.

4.nervousness or irritability.

5.tiredness or muscle weakness.

6.trembling hands.

Causes

Researchers aren’t sure why some people develop autoimmune disorders such as Graves’ disease.

These disorders probably develop from a combination of genes and an outside trigger, such as a virus.

1.With Graves’ disease, the immune system makes an antibody called thyroid-stimulating immunoglobulin (TSI) that attaches to thyroid cells.

2.TSI acts like thyroid-stimulating hormone (TSH), a hormone made in the pituitary gland that tells the thyroid how much thyroid hormone to make. TSI causes the thyroid to make too much thyroid hormone.

Diagnosis of Graves’ Disease

To diagnose Graves' disease, your doctor may conduct a physical exam and check for signs and symptoms of Graves' disease.

1.To diagnose Graves' disease, your doctor may conduct a physical exam and check for signs and symptoms of Graves' disease.

2.Radioactive iodine uptake.

3.Ultrasound.

4.Imaging tests.

Treatment of Graves’ Disease

Treatment options for grave's disease include:

1.Radioiodine therapy

2.Beta blockers.

3.Antithyroid medicines.
